# Engagement Findings Memo — NYC 311 Public Services

**Date:** 2026-03-19
**Sources:** Comptroller audit (DOB), Mayor's Management Report (FY2024), NYS Comptroller monitoring tool

---

## 1. Reactive, Complaint-Driven Enforcement

The Department of Buildings (DOB) relies almost exclusively on 311 complaints to trigger enforcement actions for one- and two-family properties. There is no risk-based prioritization or proactive inspection model.

- 74,035 complaints received and 71,954 inspections attempted (2022-2023).
- DOB cannot determine whether high-complaint communities are actually the highest-violation communities.
- The anonymous 311 system has no safeguards against targeted or abusive filing.

**Implication:** Enforcement activity reflects complaint generation patterns — driven by awareness and digital access — rather than actual building code risk.

---

## 2. Equity Gaps in Penalties

Penalties fall disproportionately on lower-income and minority communities.

- 7 of the top 10 penalty districts have median household incomes below the citywide median (\$76,607).
- Black households are the most prevalent group in 7 of the top 10 penalty districts; Hispanic households rank in the top 2 in 6 of 10.
- Total penalties on one- and two-family properties: \$38.3M (21% of \$186M citywide).
- Default judgments account for 63% (\$24.3M) of those penalties, imposed on only 21% of violations — meaning non-response (likely inability to respond) drives the bulk of financial burden.

---

## 3. Illegal Conversions as a Penalty Multiplier

Illegal conversion violations carry outsized financial consequences.

- 116 illegal conversion violations (37% of 314 total among top-penalty properties) generated \$3.9M — 79% of penalties for those properties.
- A single violation can reach \$70,000 (base + default + daily penalties).
- 40 violations resulted in default judgments totaling \$2.04M.

---

## 4. Plan Review Bottlenecks

DOB plan approval times have increased 80% citywide as application volume rose 106%.

- Q1 2024 citywide average for major alterations: 124 business days.
- Queens saw a 93% increase (57.9 to 112.1 days); Brooklyn 87% (69.7 to 130.6 days).
- Plans sat with applicants for ~70% of the review period; DOB's own review consumed ~30% (avg 34 business days).
- Homeowners were issued "Failure to Comply" violations while their plans were still pending DOB approval — enforcement outpacing the agency's own processing.

---

## 5. Follow-Through Failures

The correction/re-inspection pipeline is broken.

- 9,167 Requests for Corrective Action issued.
- Only 19% confirmed corrected; 14% escalated to OATH violations.
- **60% remained open with no evidence of re-inspection** — the majority of cases simply fall into a void.

---

## 6. 311 Channel Shift and Capacity Strain

The 311 system is handling more volume through digital channels while phone service degrades.

- FY2024: 38.2M total contacts (18.2M web, 17.5M calls, 2.5M mobile/text/social).
- Web visits up 21% year-over-year; call volume declining steadily (21.5M in FY20 to 17.5M in FY24).
- **Call answer rate dropped from 85% to 74%**, missing the 80% target by 6 points.
- Average wait time nearly doubled: 17 seconds (FY23) to 31 seconds (FY24).
- Staffing is flat (379 actual vs. 396 planned); expenditures up to \$61.9M.

---

## 7. Data Quality and Measurement Caveats

Several structural issues limit what 311 data can reliably tell us.

- **Duplicate requests:** A single issue can generate 3-5+ entries, inflating complaint counts.
- **Reporting bias:** Neighborhoods with higher digital access and 311 awareness generate more complaints per capita regardless of actual conditions.
- **Non-resident complaints:** Commercial/tourist areas include filings from non-residents.
- **Resolution tracking gaps:** Some requests are closed administratively without action; others remain open indefinitely.
- **Complaint co-occurrence:** Areas with high HEAT/HOT WATER complaints also show high PLUMBING and PAINT/PLASTER rates — suggesting building-level maintenance failures, not isolated issues.

---

## Cross-Cutting Themes

| Theme | Evidence |
|---|---|
| **Enforcement lags behind its own processes** | Violations issued during pending plan reviews; 60% of corrections never re-inspected |
| **Equity is a structural problem, not incidental** | Penalty concentration in low-income/minority districts; default judgments as the main penalty driver |
| **The 311 funnel is high-volume but low-signal** | Duplicates, reporting bias, and no risk stratification mean raw complaint counts are unreliable proxies for need |
| **Agency capacity is not scaling with demand** | 80% longer plan reviews, declining call answer rates, flat headcount |
| **Complaint type determines resolution timeline** | NYPD-routed complaints resolve fast; DOB-routed complaints take weeks — the agency, not the borough, is the primary driver |
